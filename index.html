About Me

I am an applied microeconomist working at the intersection of environmental, development, and labor economics. I received my PhD in Economics from American University in May 2026, where my dissertation, Essays in Environmental and Climate Economics, was advised by Mieke Meurs, Natalia Radchenko, and Boris Gershman.


My research examines how air pollution, climate shocks, and environmental policy shape labor supply, child outcomes, and intra-household decisions in South Asia. I combine applied microeconometrics with spatial and remote-sensing data, building measures of environmental exposure from satellite imagery and geospatial sources.

My job market paper, Clearing the Air: How Pollution and Policy Shape Labor Supply in Bangladesh, studies how air pollution and environmental policy affect labor supply decisions.

Research interests: Environmental and Climate Economics, Development Economics, Labor and Demographic Economics


I was a William J. Fulbright PhD Fellow (2020–25) and have worked as a consultant with the World Bank's Social Protection and Labor and Digital Development teams.

Featured Work

Job Market Paper

Clearing the Air: How Pollution and Policy Shape Labor Supply in Bangladesh

How do air pollution and environmental policy shape who works and how much? Using spatial data on PM2.5 exposure combined with household survey data from Bangladesh, this paper traces the effects of pollution and policy on labor supply.
